One of the first and largest inspirations in my scientific and engineering career was my discovery of Linux. The learning curve was steep, as it often is for complicated technical systems, but after I got the grasp of it my world was widened. The benefits of Linux have been great for me: I have a huge selection of free software to use and learn from, as my command line experience grew so did my productivity and intuition for coding, I have a seemingly-infinite playground to creatively develop the computer that I want to use, not one that is given to me in some pre-defined, locked state, and I can make a kick-ass computer using old hardware for very little money. It's awesome how many times my ten-year-old Linux machine has outperformed Cornell University's brand new Windows machines or my friends' $2000 Macbooks. After some time, I moved from the Ubuntu distribution to Arch Linux, which has been my platform of choice for the last few years.

Linux also opened me up to the larger world of open-source software and hardware, the philosophy of which has really influenced me. I love the idea of sharing knowledge and contributing my efforts to projects which benefit many people and are accessible at no cost. I learned about Arduino and physical computing through Linux, as well as Raspberry Pi and embedded computing.
