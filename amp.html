Overview

During my senior year at Cornell University, I undertook the ambitious project of building a class D amplifier from scratch. Well, my construction was fully from scratch, but my design was based off of the IRS2092 half-bridge driver IC. With the help of the chip's documentation and the manufacturer's reference design, I was able to apply my prior electronics experience and my engineering education to build a powerful and functional amplifier.

This photo is of my completed amplifier on top of a speaker cabinet that I also built. The knobs from left to right are: pre-amp gain, low, mid, and treble EQs, and power-amp volume.

This photo is of the finished power supply and preamplifier installed in the case.

I wanted to build a class D amplifier partly for the novelty of it, which comes mostly from the fact that most people, engineers included, don't even know what class D means. I was also drawn to the challenge of building this kind of device, because very few amatuers have done so and there are very few examples of class D amps on the web. There are more tangible benefits to this kind of amplifier, which stem almost entirely from the increased efficiency of class D operation. These benefits include lower heat dissipation, so the output transistors don't require huge heatsinks that cost a lot and take up a lot of room. Similarly, in comparison to more typical class A or class AB amplifiers, a smaller power supply can be used to achieve an equivalent power ouput to the speaker load, which drastically reduces the weight and cost of parts. There are downsides to a class D amp too. The high switching frequency introduces some hum on the output, and the clipping characteristics are really nasty.

Here is a close up of the preamplifier I built based on Rod Elliott's design.

Here is a photo of the completed power amplifier section. The output filter is composed of the large black inductor and blue capacitor on the left. The 16 pin IC is the IRS2092 gate driver. The 5 pin TO-92 package on the left is the half-bridge, and the other TO-92 packages are voltage regulators for the input section and the gate-driver floating power supply.

I learned a lot while building this device. I'd never worked with power electronics before, so it was edifying to construct a rather high voltage power supply (70 volts peak-to-peak in this case), and through my readings and the help of my advisor I became confident in my ability to choose parts that would work for this application, be safe, and be robust. After destroying a few PCB traces and blowing up a few power resistors due to short circuits and other testing mishaps, I became more aware of the power of the device I'd built, and also its capabilities and limitations too.

Another valuable take away from this project is the confidence I've gained in learning from technical documentation. On my first attempt to build the power amp, I merely copied the schematic of the reference design, and when I plugged it in I destroyed the integrated circuit. I relized that this was a big waste of time and parts because I now had no way to find where my error was, nor could I do much to fix it because all my parts were soldered onto a PCB. So, I went back to the drawing board and thoroughly read all the documentation of the IRS2092, which was great because I ended up learning how every part of the reference schematic worked. I then successfully breadboarded the amplifier and ran it off of a lower voltage benchtop power supply before soldering the parts to my re-designed PCB.

The build also taught me the importance of focusing on attention to detail from the very beginning of a big project like this. There were times when I would be stuck on an issue for a few days that turned out to be that a part was installed the wrong way!! That is frustrating, but I think I've learned my lesson.

Of course, I also learned a lot more about construction through this project. I drilled and milled the aluminum case in Cornell's student machine shop, and I learned a lot about schematic capture and PCB design in KiCad. My soldering skills were practiced during many late nights.
